Squats Aren't Bad for Your Knees

Almost everyone has heard it: don't squat below parallel, it's terrible for your knees. Coaches repeat it, gym posters illustrate it, and well-meaning people pass it along as obvious common sense. The deep squat became the movement you were supposed to fear. But when you actually look at the biomechanics and the long-term data, the warning gets it close to backwards — and the "safe" shallow squat people retreat to isn't the safer option they think it is.

Where the fear came from

The anti-deep-squat idea traces back to old, limited research and a reasonable-sounding intuition: the deeper you go, the more your knees bend, so surely the deeper you go, the more they're stressed and the more likely they are to be damaged. It feels logical. The problem is that "more knee bend" and "more joint damage" are not the same thing, and the forces inside the knee don't behave the way the intuition assumes.

A detailed biomechanical review of squat depth examined exactly this question and concluded that the deep squat, performed with good technique, does not carry an increased risk of knee injury and is not inherently dangerous to the joint.[1] Decades of using deep squats in trained populations simply don't show the epidemic of ruined knees the warning predicts.

The forces don't peak where you'd guess

Here's the part that flips the story. The compressive force behind the kneecap — the retropatellar force people worry about — does not just keep climbing the deeper you go. It tends to peak around the half-squat, roughly 90 degrees of knee flexion, and then the geometry of the joint changes as you descend further.[1] As the knee bends past that point, surrounding soft tissue contacts and a "wrapping effect" helps distribute load across a larger area, rather than concentrating it.

In other words, the half-squat — the depth a lot of people adopt because they think it's gentler — is sitting near the worst spot for kneecap stress, not away from it.

The shallow squat people choose to protect their knees often loads the kneecap more, not less, than going all the way down.

The hidden cost of cutting depth

There's a second problem with chopping the squat short. Because partial squats feel easier, people pile on far more weight to compensate — quarter and half squats with supramaximal loads. That heavy, shallow loading pushes large compressive and shear forces through the spine and the rest of the kinetic chain.[1] So the "knee-friendly" choice can quietly trade a knee worry you didn't actually have for spinal loading you didn't intend.

Meanwhile, the full-depth squat done with a load you can actually control trains the muscles through a longer range, which is exactly what builds them, and keeps the weight at a level your structure can handle.

Technique is the real variable

None of this means depth is a free-for-all. The reviews are clear that the protective picture assumes good technique and appropriate loading.[1] What actually matters for squatting safely is well-understood: control the descent rather than dropping into the bottom, keep the load within what your form can support, keep the bar tracking over your midfoot, and build depth gradually as your mobility and confidence allow.[2] A clean deep squat is safe; a sloppy one at any depth is the thing to avoid.

The honest caveats

This is a general claim about healthy knees, not a blanket medical clearance. If you have an existing knee condition, a past surgery, or pain that shows up when you squat, that's a conversation for a physio or doctor, and they may have good reasons to limit your depth for a while. Mobility matters too — if you can't reach depth without your heels lifting or your back rounding, the fix is building that mobility, not forcing a position your body can't hold yet. And "deep is safe" specifically means controlled depth with a manageable load. Crashing into the bottom with a bar you can't handle is a different thing entirely.

The honest summary

The fear of deep squats is built on intuition that the biomechanics don't support. A full squat with good technique doesn't increase knee injury risk, kneecap stress actually peaks around the half-squat depth many people retreat to, and loading up heavy partial squats shifts force onto your spine. Technique and sensible loading are what keep you safe — not the height you stop at. If your knees are healthy and your form is solid, squatting deep is a feature, not a hazard.
