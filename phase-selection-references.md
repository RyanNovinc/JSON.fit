# Phase Selection & Body Composition Research References

The phase-selection, rate, surplus/deficit, and protein recommendations in JSON.fit are grounded in peer-reviewed research where it exists, and in clearly-labelled practitioner models where it does not. This page documents the studies and how they inform the guidance.

For the practical guidance applied to plans, see [phase-selection.md](https://json.fit/phase-selection.md).

(The lean-mass math is a separate document with its own references: [lean-mass-targets.md](https://json.fit/lean-mass-targets.md) + [lean-mass-targets-references.md](https://json.fit/lean-mass-targets-references.md).)

## Protein Intake for Muscle Gain and Retention

### The Anchor Meta-Analysis
The largest meta-analysis on the topic pooled 49 studies and 1863 participants (randomised controlled trials, resistance training ≥6 weeks). Protein supplementation significantly increased fat-free mass (+0.30 kg, 95% CI 0.09–0.52) and 1-RM strength (+2.49 kg). A breakpoint analysis found total protein intakes beyond ~1.62 g/kg/day produced no further fat-free-mass gains, with the confidence interval extending to ~2.2 g/kg/day — the widely-cited defensible upper bound. The effect was larger in trained individuals and smaller with increasing age.

[Morton et al. 2018 — Br J Sports Med 52(6):376–384](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5867436/) · doi:10.1136/bjsports-2017-097608

**Application in JSON.fit:** protein target 1.6–2.2 g/kg bodyweight across all phases. The upper end is used on a cut, where protein needs rise to protect lean mass and support satiety (see deficit section).

## Rate of Fat Loss and Deficit Size

### The Rate-of-Loss RCT
24 elite athletes were randomised to a slow (0.7%/wk) or fast (1.4%/wk) rate of weight loss while performing 4 resistance sessions/week. Both groups lost similar fat. The slow group *gained* lean body mass (+2.1%, p<0.001); the fast group did not (−0.2%), a significant between-group difference (p<0.01). This is the primary evidence for a target rate of ~0.5–1% of bodyweight per week.

[Garthe et al. 2011 — Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab 21(2):97–104](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21558571/)

### The Deficit-Magnitude Meta-Analysis
A meta-analysis and meta-regression of RCTs of resistance training in an energy deficit found lean-mass gains were impaired versus a non-deficit control (effect size −0.57, p=0.02), while strength gains were comparable (ES −0.31, p=0.28, non-significant). The meta-regression identified a deficit of ~500 kcal/day as the point beyond which lean-mass gains were prevented. An earlier companion analysis found ≤500 kcal/day deficits maintained lean mass in untrained individuals.

[Murphy & Koehler 2022 — Scand J Med Sci Sports 32(1):125–137](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34623696/) · doi:10.1111/sms.14075

**Application in JSON.fit:** cut deficit capped at ~500 kcal/day; recomp uses maintenance-to-small deficit (<~400 kcal/day). Both bias protein high.

## Energy Surplus Size for a Bulk

### The Review That Named the Gap
A review synthesising the (limited) direct evidence plus overfeeding studies concluded that the specific energy surplus required to maximise hypertrophy has never been validated in a resistance-training population, and that overfeeding *alone* (without training) preferentially adds fat, not fat-free mass. A large surplus is not required for muscle gain.

[Slater et al. 2019 — Front Nutr 6:131](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31482093/) · doi:10.3389/fnut.2019.00131

### The Direct Surplus RCT
21 trained lifters (17 completers after COVID attrition) were randomised to maintenance, moderate (+5%), or high (+15%) energy surplus for 8 weeks. Faster body-mass gain (the larger surplus) primarily increased fat gain rather than augmenting strength or muscle thickness (a modest biceps effect aside). The study was underpowered by its planned sample, so it constrains rather than pinpoints the optimal surplus.

[Helms et al. 2023 — Sports Med Open 9:102](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37914977/) · doi:10.1186/s40798-023-00651-y

**Application in JSON.fit:** a small surplus (~+5% lean bulk, up to ~+10% bulk) is used, scaled down with training experience. The exact percentage is a practitioner target, not a validated optimum — the evidence supports the *direction* (small is enough; large just adds fat), not a precise number.

## Body Recomposition (Simultaneous Muscle Gain + Fat Loss)

### The Review
A narrative review documented body recomposition not only in novice and overweight populations but also in resistance-trained individuals, identifying progressive resistance training plus adequate protein as the key levers. It is easiest in those new to training, returning after a layoff, or carrying higher body fat.

[Barakat et al. 2020 — Strength Cond J 42(5):7–21](https://journals.lww.com/nsca-scj/fulltext/2020/10000/body_recomposition__can_trained_individuals_build.3.aspx) · doi:10.1519/SSC.0000000000000584

A supporting RCT (Longland et al. 2016, Am J Clin Nutr) found that a higher-protein diet during an energy deficit combined with intense training produced simultaneous lean-mass gain and fat-mass loss in young men.

**Application in JSON.fit:** recomp is offered as a recommended phase for new, returning, and higher-body-fat users — not for lean, well-trained lifters, in whom it is slow.

## Muscle Memory / Detrained Returners

### The Phenomenon
Previously-trained individuals regain muscle size and strength faster than the untrained. In a training–detraining–retraining study, retraining produced a larger lean-mass increase than the initial training period, accompanied by retained DNA hypomethylation of growth-related genes ("epigenetic memory").

[Seaborne et al. 2018 — Sci Rep 8:1898 (epigenetic memory of hypertrophy)] · [Cumming et al. 2024 — J Physiol, doi:10.1113/JP285675 (myonuclear permanence)](https://physoc.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1113/JP285675)

### The Contested Mechanism
The *observation* that detrained lifters regain quickly is reasonably supported. The *mechanism* — permanent myonuclear accretion — is genuinely debated: a systematic review/meta-analysis found conflicting evidence, with some human and rodent studies showing myonuclei are lost during prolonged inactivity.

[Myonuclear permanence — systematic review & meta-analysis 2022 — PMC9530508](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9530508/)

**Application in JSON.fit:** a "returning after a break" training-state option routes toward recomp/regain framing. Framed as a faster-regain *phenomenon*, not a claim about a specific cellular mechanism.

## Diet Breaks on Longer Cuts

### The Diet-Break RCT
51 men with obesity were randomised to 16 weeks of continuous energy restriction, or intermittent restriction delivered as alternating 2-week deficit / 2-week maintenance blocks. The intermittent group achieved greater weight and fat loss and a smaller (adaptive-thermogenesis) drop in resting energy expenditure.

[Byrne et al. 2018 (MATADOR) — Int J Obes 42(2):129–138](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28925405/) · doi:10.1038/ijo.2017.206

**Application in JSON.fit:** a 1–2 week maintenance break is suggested on cuts running beyond ~8–12 weeks. Note the trial was in obese men, not lean lifters, and used a fixed 2-week alternating structure — the "every 8–12 weeks" cadence is a practitioner extrapolation.

## Body Fat Level and Muscle Gain (the "P-Ratio" Question)

This is the most contested area in this document, and the guidance is written to respect that.

The traditional strong claim — that you must get lean before bulking, or that being leaner "potentiates" hypertrophy and makes gains leaner — is **not supported by direct evidence.** An analysis by Trexler and Nuckols (Stronger by Science) reviewing the indirect and cross-sectional literature found no compelling evidence that baseline body-fat percentage predicts a worse ratio of lean-to-fat gain in trained lifters, and if anything a slightly permissive trend at higher body fat.

[Trexler & Nuckols — "Should You Cut Before You Bulk?" (Stronger by Science, analysis, not a primary RCT)](https://www.strongerbyscience.com/p-ratios/)

However, this analysis is **debated.** Henselmans published a detailed rebuttal arguing the supporting literature is too indirect and confounded, and proposing an inverted-U (impairment likely only at high body fat, with no effect across the common healthy range).

[Henselmans — rebuttal](https://mennohenselmans.com/optimal-body-fat-muscle-growth/) · [Trexler — response](https://www.strongerbyscience.com/p-ratios-rebuttal/)

**Where they agree:** across a normal, healthy body-fat range (roughly ≤20–25% for men), there is no compelling evidence that body-fat percentage meaningfully impairs muscle gain, and no evidence that cutting first improves future gains. Reasons to get leaner are health, aesthetics, recovery, and keeping a later cut short — not an inability to build muscle.

**Application in JSON.fit:** the "no cut-first mandate" rule in phase-selection.md rests on the *agreed* conclusion (cutting first does not potentiate hypertrophy), not on the stronger contested claim. The guidance does not tell users body fat is irrelevant; it tells them cutting first is optional and about preference/health.

## Maximum Fat-Loss Rate (why leaner = slower)

### The Theoretical Model
A theoretical model derived from Minnesota Starvation Experiment data estimated the maximum rate energy can be drawn from fat stores at ~290 kJ/kg/day (~31 kcal per lb of fat per day; the author later privately corrected this toward ~22 kcal/lb, unpublished). The practical implication: the leaner a person is, the lower their *absolute* ceiling on daily fat loss, so cut targets should scale to fat mass.

[Alpert 2005 — J Theor Biol 233(1):1–13](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15615615/) · doi:10.1016/j.jtbi.2004.08.029

**Application in JSON.fit:** cut rate is expressed as % of bodyweight and reduced as the user leans out. Note this is a single theoretical model, not a direct experimental measurement.

## Rate of Muscle Gain by Training Status

The tiered monthly rates (new ~1–1.5%/mo of bodyweight, intermediate ~0.5–1%/mo, advanced ~0.25–0.5%/mo) come from **practitioner models** (Lyle McDonald; Alan Aragon), not from controlled trials. No single RCT establishes these exact figures. They are broadly consistent with the observed "newbie gains" literature (large first-year gains that roughly halve each subsequent year) and with the Morton finding that trained lifters respond less to added protein than novices, but they should be treated as planning heuristics.

Lyle McDonald — [genetic muscular potential model](https://bodyrecomposition.com/muscle-gain/genetic-muscular-potential) (practitioner). Alan Aragon — percentage-of-bodyweight model (practitioner).

**Application in JSON.fit:** these rates set the *ceiling* used to scale the bulk surplus by training state and to set user expectations. Labelled as estimates, not guarantees.

## Confidence Assessment

### Well-Supported (RCT or meta-analytic evidence)
- Protein ~1.6 g/kg/day maximises training-induced fat-free-mass gains; up to ~2.2 g/kg is a defensible ceiling (Morton 2018)
- A slower rate of weight loss (~0.5–1%/wk) preserves — and can build — lean mass better than a fast rate (Garthe 2011)
- Deficits beyond ~500 kcal/day increasingly cost lean mass; ≤500 kcal/day retains it (Murphy & Koehler 2022)
- A large surplus mainly adds fat rather than extra muscle (Slater 2019; Helms 2023 — direction robust, magnitude underpowered)
- Body recomposition is achievable, most readily in novice/returning/higher-body-fat individuals (Barakat 2020; Longland 2016)
- Diet breaks improve fat-loss efficiency and blunt metabolic adaptation in obese men (Byrne 2018)

### Practitioner Extrapolation (reasoned, not directly RCT-validated)
- The exact bulk surplus percentages (+5%/+10%) and their scaling by training state
- Monthly muscle-gain rate tiers by training age (Lyle/Aragon models)
- The "every 8–12 weeks, 1–2 week diet break" cadence for lean lifters
- Applying the ~500 kcal deficit finding, largely from untrained/short studies, to trained lifters

### Contested / Unsettled
- Whether body-fat percentage impairs muscle gain at the high end, and the shape of that relationship (linear vs inverted-U) — Trexler/Nuckols vs Henselmans
- The cellular mechanism of muscle memory (myonuclear permanence) — supported by some studies, disputed by others

### Not Supported
- That you must cut to a low body fat before bulking in order to build muscle
- That getting lean first "potentiates" future hypertrophy or guarantees leaner gains

## Important Caveats

1. **Most muscle-gain and recomp rate figures are heuristics, not measured constants.** Individual variation is large (±30–40%), and genetics, sleep, and training quality dominate.

2. **The deficit and diet-break evidence skews toward untrained or obese populations and short durations.** Generalisation to lean, trained lifters is reasonable but not proven.

3. **The surplus RCT (Helms 2023) was underpowered by COVID attrition.** It supports "small surplus is enough," not a precise optimal percentage.

4. **Consumer body-fat readings (bioimpedance, and even DEXA) carry several points of error.** Every body-fat threshold in the guidance should be applied to *trends* (scale weight, waist, photos, strength), not a single reading.

5. **The p-ratio literature is genuinely debated.** JSON.fit's guidance deliberately rests only on the agreed conclusion (cutting first doesn't potentiate hypertrophy), not on the stronger contested claim.

6. **None of this is medical advice, and it is not for people with eating disorders.** Users with a history of disordered eating should not be steered toward aggressive deficits, narrowed windows, or rigid body-fat targeting.

---

## Bottom Line

The strongest evidence is unglamorous and mostly about *not* doing dumb things: keep protein adequate (~1.6–2.2 g/kg), lose weight slowly (~0.5–1%/wk), don't run deficits much past ~500 kcal/day if you want to keep muscle, and don't over-eat on a bulk because a big surplus just adds fat. Body recomposition is real for the right person (novice, returning, or higher-body-fat). The "you must cut first to build muscle" rule is not supported and should not be told to users. The exact phase percentages and rate tiers are reasonable practitioner targets, not validated constants — which is why the app treats them as adjustable defaults and reads trends, not single measurements.
