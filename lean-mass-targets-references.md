# Lean-Mass Targets Research References

The lean-mass-target and "overshoot then cut" logic in JSON.fit rests on body-composition arithmetic plus the physiology of how the body partitions weight change. This page documents the basis and the measurement limits.

For the practical guidance applied to plans, see [lean-mass-targets.md](https://json.fit/lean-mass-targets.md).

---

## The Lean-Mass Formula Is Definitional

leanMass = weight × (1 − bodyFat%). This is arithmetic, not an empirical finding — it is true by the definition of body-fat percentage. No citation is required for the formula itself; the citations below concern *why lean mass is the right durable target* and *why the landing point cannot be promised exactly*.

---

## Why Weight Change Partitions the Way It Does (Forbes' Theory)

The ratio of fat-free mass to fat mass gained or lost during an energy imbalance is not fixed — it depends on how much fat a person is carrying. Forbes described this relationship, and Hall re-derived it mathematically: leaner individuals lose proportionally more fat-free mass (and, in a surplus, gain proportionally more as lean), whereas fatter individuals lose proportionally more fat. This is the physiological basis for treating lean mass as the durable target and for the "bulk past goal weight, then cut" logic — the fat accumulated on a bulk is expected, and is stripped on the cut while lean mass is largely retained.

[Hall 2007 — Forbes's theory revisited — Br J Nutr 97(6):1059–1063](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0007114507691946) · doi:10.1017/S0007114507691946

Note: Forbes' curve is also the theoretical backdrop to the body-fat / muscle-gain ("p-ratio") debate documented in [phase-selection-references.md](https://json.fit/phase-selection-references.md); the practical takeaways there and here are consistent.

## Why You Must Not Promise an Exact Landing (Measurement Error)

Every body-fat number a user provides — and every one a plan targets — carries real error, so an exact "you will be X kg at Y%" is not defensible.

- **DEXA** (a research-grade reference) still carries a standard error of roughly 2.5–3.5 percentage points of body fat, with test-retest precision around ±0.5–2 points under good conditions.
- **Bioelectrical impedance (BIA)** — smart scales, handheld devices, and the Evolt/InBody class — typically carries ±3–10 percentage points versus DEXA and is sensitive to hydration, recent food, and recent exercise. A single reading can be off by several points; individual agreement with DEXA is poor even when group-level correlation is high.

[BIA vs DEXA method comparison — PMC9347159](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9347159/) · [DEXA standard error of measurement (Dexalytics summary of Gatterer et al.)](https://www.dexalytics.com/news/higher-values-dxa/)

**Application in JSON.fit:** body-fat targets are applied to *trends*, not single readings, and the guidance forbids promising an exact scale-weight-at-body-fat landing. Bulk targets are expressed as lean mass; the cut reveals the result.

## Confidence Assessment

### Well-Supported
- Body-fat measurement carries several percentage points of error (DEXA best-case ~2.5–3.5; BIA ±3–10) — consistent across method-comparison studies
- The lean-mass formula is definitional (true by construction)

### Reasoned / Theoretical
- Forbes' partitioning theory (Hall 2007) — a validated mathematical description of group-level body-composition change, applied here as individual-level framing
- The "overshoot then cut" strategy — a logical consequence of partitioning theory plus the measurement limits, not a directly trialled protocol

---

## Bottom Line

Track lean mass, not scale weight, as the durable target — it follows directly from the definition of body-fat percentage and from how the body partitions weight change (Forbes/Hall). But because every body-fat reading carries several points of error, never promise an exact landing: bulk toward a lean-mass figure, read the trend, and let the cut reveal the final composition.
