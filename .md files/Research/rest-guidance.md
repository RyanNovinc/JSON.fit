# Rest Period Guidance

Research-backed rest periods between sets, organized by exercise category and user-selected rest tier.

The user's rest tier comes from their questionnaire selection: Optimal, Moderate, or Minimal. Apply the appropriate column based on their selection. Within each tier, rest periods vary by exercise category.

---

## Per-Tier × Exercise Category Matrix

| Exercise Category | Optimal | Moderate | Minimal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heavy compound (squat, deadlift, bench, OHP, weighted pull-ups/dips at <6 RM) | 3–5 min | 2.5–3 min | 2 min |
| Moderate compound (rows, machine compounds, lat pulldowns, leg press, hack squat) | 2.5–3 min | 2 min | 75–90 s |
| Unilateral compound (Bulgarian splits, lunges, single-arm rows) | 90 s – 2 min between sides; 2.5 min between pairs | 75–90 s between sides; 2 min between pairs | 60 s between sides; 90 s between pairs |
| Large-muscle isolation (curls, triceps extensions, leg curls, leg extensions, pec deck) | 2 min | 75–90 s | 60 s |
| Small-muscle isolation (lateral raises, rear delts, calves, wrist curls, face pulls) | 60–90 s | 45–75 s | 30–60 s |

---

## Tier Definitions

### Optimal
Full recovery between every set. Prioritises stimulus per set. Reproduces the rest intervals with strongest peer-reviewed support for both hypertrophy and strength in trained lifters. Trade-off: longer total session duration.

### Moderate
Balanced recovery. Aligns with the most current meta-analytic evidence (Singer 2024) showing hypertrophy benefits plateau around 90 s. Accepts a small potential trade-off in maximum strength outcomes for trained lifters on heaviest compounds in exchange for shorter sessions.

### Minimal
Time-efficient. Does NOT go below 90–120 s on heavy compounds. Below that, robust evidence shows meaningful loss of strength gains. Pair this tier with antagonist supersets on natural pairs (chest/back, biceps/triceps, hamstrings/quads isolation, side delts/face pulls) for additional time savings without compromising adaptations.

---

## Goal-Specific Adjustments

### Hypertrophy
Use the matrix above. Benefits plateau around 90 s for direct hypertrophy outcomes. Set-to-set rep performance is the corrective signal: if reps drop more than 15–20% from set 1 to set 2 at the same load, rest was too short.

### Strength (max 1–5 RM)
Override the matrix. Heavy compound lifts require 3–5 min regardless of tier — short rest significantly compromises 1RM gains in trained lifters. For singles or doubles in advanced lifters, 5–7 min is appropriate. This is the only goal where 3–5 min is non-negotiable on heavy compounds.

### Body Recomposition
Use the matrix as for hypertrophy. There is no rest interval that has been shown to specifically optimise body recomposition. Protect the hypertrophic stimulus through longer rest; create energy deficit through diet and separate cardio, not by truncating rest.

### Fat Loss
Use the matrix as for hypertrophy. Compressed-rest "metabolic resistance training" does increase acute caloric expenditure but trades off strength and hypertrophy adaptations. Better to keep traditional rest and add separate cardio if greater energy expenditure is the goal.

### General Fitness
60–120 s across all categories is fully sufficient. Supersets become attractive — comparable adaptations in roughly half the session time.

## Edge Cases

### Antagonist Supersets (chest/back rows, biceps/triceps, leg curl/leg extension, lateral raise/face pull)
Rest 30–60 s between the two exercises in the antagonist pair, then 90 s – 2 min before the next round. Evidence shows antagonist supersets preserve repetitions and volume load versus traditional sets while delivering significantly greater training efficiency. Subgroup analysis: antagonist supersets actually allow MORE total reps than traditional sets.

### Same-Muscle Supersets (e.g., bench press + dumbbell press)
Avoid for hypertrophy in trained lifters. Meta-analytic evidence shows reduced volume load. Acceptable as a finisher only.

### Drop Sets
Within the drop sequence, rest is the time it takes to change the load (≤10 s). Between full drop-set "rounds," apply the normal rest period for that exercise category. Hypertrophy and strength outcomes are similar to traditional sets, with substantial time savings (54–70% reduction reported).

### AMRAP / Sets to Failure
Add ~1 minute to the normal rest interval after a set taken to true failure on a compound lift; ~30 s extra on an isolation. Failure does not appreciably enhance hypertrophy over non-failure when volume is matched, but if the user does train to failure, recovery before the next set should be extended.

### Deload Weeks
Rest the same or slightly longer between sets (2–3 min) but use lighter loads, fewer sets, or both. The rest interval is not the lever being manipulated — load and volume are.

### Set-to-Set Rest Progression
The largest decline in repetition performance occurs between sets 1 and 2, with smaller declines from set 2 → 3 → 4. Two acceptable approaches:
- For hypertrophy: rep targets may drop slightly on later sets, OR rest may extend by 15–30 s on later sets
- For strength (heavy 3–5 RM): allow rest to grow naturally — set 2 may need 3 min, set 4 may need 5 min

If the user cannot hit prescribed reps on set 2 with planned rest, the rest is too short for that exercise on that day. Use as a feedback signal.

## Operating Principle

Rest interval is a **secondary lever** behind volume, load, proximity-to-failure, and exercise selection. Apply tier defaults, but the dominant adaptation drivers are:

1. Total weekly volume per muscle (see volume-landmarks.md)
2. Proximity to failure / RIR (see rir-guidance.md)
3. Progressive overload over time
4. Exercise selection appropriate to goal

Within ±30 s of these recommended values, no published RCT demonstrates a meaningful adaptation difference.
