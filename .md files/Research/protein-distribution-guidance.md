# Protein Distribution Guidance

Research-backed guidance for distributing protein across meals to support muscle protein synthesis and hypertrophy.

This guidance assumes total daily protein is already adequate (≥1.6 g/kg/day for trained lifters). Distribution is a SECONDARY optimization — total daily protein dominates outcomes. Apply this guidance once daily totals are set.

---

## Per-Meal Protein Targets

| Body Weight | Minimum (0.4 g/kg) | Upper Range (0.55 g/kg) |
|---|---|---|
| 60 kg | 24 g | 33 g |
| 70 kg | 28 g | 38 g |
| 80 kg | 32 g | 44 g |
| 90 kg | 36 g | 50 g |
| 100 kg | 40 g | 55 g |

Each main meal should hit at least 0.4 g/kg of high-quality protein. The 0.55 g/kg upper end accommodates higher daily protein totals (up to 2.2 g/kg) distributed across 4 meals.

For users above 25% body fat, calculations using lean body mass are more physiologically defensible — but for most users, total bodyweight scaling is adequate.

## Meal Frequency Mapping

The strongest acute mechanistic evidence supports 3–5 protein-containing meals spaced 3–5 hours apart. Chronic hypertrophy outcomes are less sensitive to frequency — anywhere from 2–6 meals can work if total protein is adequate.

### 2 meals/day
**Verdict:** Workable but theoretically suboptimal for peak muscle protein synthesis.
**Per-meal target:** 0.7–1.1 g/kg (50–80 g for a 70 kg lifter)
**Notes:** Recent research (Trommelen 2023) shows large boluses produce a more prolonged anabolic response than previously thought, so the practical penalty is likely smaller than older models suggested. Suitable for time-restricted eating protocols if total daily protein is met.

### 3 meals/day
**Verdict:** Standard, well-supported pattern.
**Per-meal target:** 0.5–0.7 g/kg (35–50 g for 70 kg)
**Notes:** Most widely studied real-world pattern. Mamerow 2014 (even distribution) and Yasuda 2020 (12-week RCT) both support even distribution across 3 meals.

### 4 meals/day
**Verdict:** Theoretical optimum from acute MPS data.
**Per-meal target:** 0.4–0.55 g/kg (28–38 g for 70 kg)
**Notes:** Aligns with Areta 2013 (4×20 g produced 31% greater myofibrillar FSR than 8×10 g pulse and 48% greater than 2×40 g bolus). The Schoenfeld & Aragon 2018 default recommendation.

### 5 meals/day
**Verdict:** Useful for high daily protein intakes; otherwise no clear advantage over 4.
**Per-meal target:** 0.32–0.44 g/kg (22–31 g for 70 kg)
**Notes:** Risk of dipping below the per-meal floor at lower daily totals. Best for high-volume bodybuilding diets where 4 meals would be uncomfortably large.

### 6 meals/day
**Verdict:** No clear MPS or hypertrophy advantage over 4 meals.
**Per-meal target:** 0.27–0.37 g/kg (19–26 g for 70 kg)
**Notes:** Largely convenience and bodybuilding tradition. Areta 2013 found 8 meals at 1.5h spacing was inferior to 4 meals at 3h spacing — suggesting too-frequent feeding may hit the muscle-full refractory window. Justified mainly by appetite or hitting very high daily totals.

## Goal-Specific Recommendations

### Hypertrophy (energy balance or surplus)
- Total: 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day
- Distribution: 4 meals at 0.4–0.55 g/kg each
- Source: any high-quality protein at each meal
- Even distribution preferred

### Body Recomposition
- Total: 1.8–2.5 g/kg/day
- Distribution: 4 meals; emphasize even distribution
- Per-meal: 0.4–0.55 g/kg
- Higher daily total protects lean mass under near-maintenance calories

### Fat Loss / Cutting
- Total: 2.2–3.1 g/kg/day for lean trained athletes
- Distribution: 3–5 meals at 0.4–0.5 g/kg each
- Higher per-meal doses matter MORE during deficit (catabolic environment raises threshold to overcome breakdown)
- Lowe 2020 TREAT trial showed ~65% of weight lost was lean mass when protein wasn't controlled — protein adequacy is critical during deficit

### Maintenance (non-training)
- Total: 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day
- Distribution: 3 meals at 0.25–0.4 g/kg sufficient

### Older Adults (anabolic resistance)
- Total: ≥1.4–1.6 g/kg/day
- Per-meal: 0.4 g/kg (~30–40 g per meal)
- Distribution matters MORE here than in young adults — each meal must independently overcome anabolic resistance
- Leucine threshold may rise to ~3–4 g per meal
- Even distribution principle especially important

## Pre-Sleep Protein

Pre-sleep casein (20–40 g, 30 minutes before bed) is mechanistically supported and does NOT conflict with meal-timing rules for sleep. The casein literature uses small (~140–500 kcal) liquid boluses, not full meals — categorically different gastric load and reflux risk profile.

**When pre-sleep casein is useful:**
- User's daily protein total isn't being met by main meals
- User trains in the evening and needs additional MPS support
- User is in caloric deficit and wants to maximize lean mass retention

**When pre-sleep casein is marginal:**
- Daily protein already ≥1.6 g/kg distributed across 4 meals (Holwerda 2018 found no chronic benefit when daily protein was already adequate)
- User has reflux issues with pre-sleep eating

**Recommended dose:** 30–40 g casein-rich protein. The 40 g dose has stronger evidence for robust overnight MPS than 20–30 g.

The honest framing: pre-sleep casein is a convenient way to add high-quality protein to a day that needs more. It's not a magic timing trick on top of an already-adequate diet.

## Post-Workout Protein

The classic "30-minute anabolic window" is NOT supported by chronic training data. The actual window is ~4–6 hours around training, and is wider when a pre-workout meal has been consumed.

**General lifter:** A normal meal containing ≥0.4 g/kg protein within 1–3 hours post-workout is sufficient.

**When timing matters more (eat sooner, within 1 hour):**
- Fasted/early-morning training (no pre-workout meal)
- Long sessions or two-a-day athletes
- Older adults (anabolic resistance)
- Endurance athletes with rapid turnaround (different mechanism)

For most lifters eating a normal pre-workout meal, post-workout protein urgency is overstated.

## Protein Source Quality

When judged by acute MPS stimulation per gram:

**Higher quality:** Whey ≥ Milk/Casein ≥ Egg > Soy > Pea/Rice > Wheat

The two key drivers are leucine content and digestion/absorption kinetics.

**Practical implications for distribution:**
- A meal hitting 0.4 g/kg with whey, milk, or lean meat will reliably exceed the 2.5 g leucine threshold
- A meal of 0.4 g/kg with predominantly plant protein (especially wheat- or rice-dominant) may fall short on leucine — scale up by ~25% or combine sources (e.g., pea + rice; legume + grain)
- For pre-sleep specifically: casein is preferred for sustained overnight aminoacidemia
- For pre-/post-workout: whey or whey-containing meals preferred for faster aminoacidemia

## Operating Principle

Protein distribution is a SECONDARY optimization behind:

1. Total daily protein adequacy (≥1.6 g/kg for trained lifters)
2. Caloric intake matching the goal
3. Resistance training stimulus
4. Sleep and recovery

The distribution recommendations above represent best-available synthesis of mechanistic data. The chronic hypertrophy evidence for distribution effects is moderate — total daily protein dominates outcomes. Apply distribution guidance once daily totals are set; don't sacrifice total intake to achieve "perfect" distribution.

A useful default: 4 meals at 0.4–0.55 g/kg each, evenly spaced 3–5 hours apart, with high-quality protein sources at each meal. Adjust meal count up or down based on user lifestyle preferences while maintaining the per-meal floor.
